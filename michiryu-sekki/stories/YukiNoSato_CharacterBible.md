# Yuki no Sato Character Bible

Canonical reference for Yuki no Sato stories, Sekki stories, Ko stories, illustrations, maps, and educational materials.

## Core Setting

Yuki no Sato is a small Edo-period village shaped by river, terrace, field, hill, forest, temple, and garden. The stories follow the year through the 24 Sekki and 72 Ko, using seasonal observation as their true central movement.

The village should feel lived-in and interdependent. Farmers, children, elders, craftspeople, cooks, fishers, travelers, and attendants each notice different parts of the year. No single person owns wisdom. The season arrives through many eyes.

The tone is quiet, reflective, humane, and hopeful. Stories are not adventure tales and should not be driven by conflict, danger, politics, warfare, or heroic conquest. Change, impermanence, attention, and community are the central forces.

## Naming Rule

Do not refer to Masaru as "the protagonist" in story materials, metadata, author notes, prompts, or future planning documents.

Use one of these names instead:

- Masaru-sensei
- Hana no Sensei
- the Flower Teacher

Use Masaru-sensei for narration, story metadata, interface labels, and character references. Use Hana no Sensei or the Flower Teacher when a respectful village title fits the scene.

"Hana no Sensei" means Flower Teacher in this canon. It is a respectful village name, not a mystical title. Masaru remains his personal name, but future story-facing materials should prefer Masaru-sensei.

## Masaru-sensei

### Complete Character Sheet

Name: Masaru

Common story-facing forms: Masaru-sensei, Hana no Sensei, the Flower Teacher

Age: About 60

Occupation: Flower teacher, ikebana practitioner, seasonal observer, occasional poet

Home: A modest house, flower studio, and seasonal garden on the edge of Yuki no Sato

Social place: Respected elder-adjacent teacher, trusted neighbor, careful listener

Core role: Guide and observer, not hero

Represents: Attention, refinement, humility, seasonal awareness

Primary story function: Masaru-sensei helps the reader notice the Ko through ikebana, village life, and the changing landscape. He often creates an arrangement inspired by the seasonal sign, but he does not always discover the sign first and does not always explain its meaning.

### Physical Description

Masaru-sensei is about sixty years old, with a lean frame kept strong by daily walking, gardening, gathering branches, sweeping, and arranging. He is old enough to be treated with respect and young enough to move steadily through fields, river paths, terraces, hillsides, temple grounds, and forest edges.

His hair is mostly gray, usually tied neatly or kept back from his face while working. His face is calm and lined around the eyes from years of outdoor observation. His hands are capable and precise: weathered from garden work, but careful when handling a blossom, reed, or branch.

He dresses plainly but with quiet refinement. His everyday clothing should be practical for Edo-period village life: muted kimono or work layers, a simple sash, straw sandals for walking, and warmer layers in winter. His clothing is cared for, mended when needed, and never showy.

His posture carries traces of disciplined education. He bows with attention, walks without hurry, and tends to pause before answering.

### Biography

Masaru-sensei is educated and refined. In his youth, he served a local lord in some capacity and received the education expected of a samurai-class man. He can read classical texts, write a steady hand, compose occasional poetry, and recognize formal etiquette.

This earlier life remains understated. Villagers know he is educated and carries himself with quiet discipline, but most simply know him as the Flower Teacher. His past should be referenced only when it deepens a seasonal or human moment. It should never pull the story toward samurai adventure, warfare, intrigue, politics, or status.

At some point before the annual cycle begins, Masaru-sensei settled on the edge of Yuki no Sato and became known for his flower studio and garden. He teaches flower arranging, studies nature, receives visitors, and creates arrangements from materials gathered in and around the village.

His deepest practice is attention. He does not command the village and does not stand above it. He learns from Hana's memory, Kenji's quick ear, Jiro's river knowledge, Yuna's farming discipline, Kiku's seasonal cooking, Haru's patience with small lives, and many others.

### Personality

Masaru-sensei is patient, humble, observant, thoughtful, kind, curious, and disciplined. He is refined without being distant. He can laugh softly, admit when he has missed something, and accept instruction from a child, farmer, fisher, or elder.

He is not preachy, mystical, or guru-like. He should not deliver grand lessons. His best insights are small, seasonal, and rooted in what has just been seen.

He prefers careful speech. When he offers an observation, it should feel like a door opening, not a verdict closing.

### Daily Routine

Before dawn, Masaru-sensei opens the shutters of his studio or steps into the garden to read the weather. He notices frost, dew, insects, bird calls, wind direction, cloud height, mud, scent, and water level before beginning the day.

Morning is for sweeping, tea, checking the garden, sharpening tools, changing water in vessels, and walking one village path. He may visit the river crossing, terrace walls, temple pond, forest edge, bridge, orchard, market baskets, or hillside depending on the Ko.

Midday is practical. He gathers material with restraint, teaches a lesson, repairs a basket, prepares containers, trims stems, records a note, or helps a neighbor with small work.

Afternoon is often spent in the studio. He studies the material gathered that day and creates an arrangement that reflects the seasonal sign. The arrangement should not merely display beauty; it should reveal timing, transition, or relationship.

Evening is quiet. He may write a short poem, drink tea with a visitor, listen to insects or rain, or leave the studio door open so villagers can see the arrangement in lamplight.

## Home and Studio

Masaru-sensei lives at the edge of Yuki no Sato where the village path begins to narrow toward fields, reeds, and low hills. His home is modest, clean, and weathered by seasons.

The house has a small entry, a simple hearth, a sleeping room, storage for tools and paper, and a veranda that looks toward the garden. The rooms are spare but warm. A kettle, writing brush, folded cloths, baskets, pruning tools, and seasonal objects may appear, but the house should never feel cluttered or wealthy.

The flower studio is attached to or just beside the home. It has sliding doors that open toward the garden, a packed-earth or wood working area, shelves for vessels, bamboo baskets, shallow suiban, tall containers, stones, twine, cloth, and cutting tools. A low table sits near the best light. Finished arrangements are placed where visitors can see them without ceremony.

The studio should feel practical rather than sacred. It is a working place: damp stems, clipped leaves, a basin of water, a bundle of reeds, a branch leaning near the wall. Beauty comes from use, care, and season.

## Garden

Masaru-sensei's garden is seasonal rather than ornamental. It includes a pine, camellia, plum, grasses, moss, a small basin that freezes in deep winter, herbs, low flowering plants, and spaces where wild things are allowed to appear.

The garden changes visibly across the 72 Ko. It should hold frost, thaw, buds, fallen petals, insects, summer heat, rain-dark leaves, seed heads, turning vines, first ice, winter stillness, and early signs of renewal.

The garden is not a grand estate garden. It is intimate, useful, and attentive. Some materials are cultivated; others arrive by wind, bird, water, or neglect. Masaru-sensei does not control every corner. That imperfection is part of its truth.

## Nearby Landscape

Masaru-sensei's home sits within walking reach of the river path, lower stream, bridge, old ferry landing, willow grove, rice terraces, southern fields, persimmon orchard, tea house, temple grounds, bamboo grove, reed beds, hillside grasslands, forest edge, and a rocky overlook.

The river is one of the village's main seasonal witnesses. The terraces reveal soil, seedlings, grain, heat, harvest, drainage, frost, and snow. The hills show wind, fog, grasses, distant weather, hawks, and migration. The forest and bamboo grove provide branches, fuel, shade, winter signs, and craft materials.

## Supporting Cast Profiles

The recurring cast below is drawn from existing Yuki no Sato story materials. Ages are canonical working ages for future consistency; keep them approximate in prose unless a story specifically needs an age.

### Hana

Age: About 82

Occupation: Elderly widow

Personality: Perceptive, steady, quietly humorous, anchored in memory

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A trusted elder and neighbor near the river crossing. She often notices changes in people as clearly as he notices changes in plants.

Continuity notes: Lives beside the river crossing and sweeps the path outside her home every morning. Her late husband may be referenced with tenderness, especially around graves, equinox, or seasonal offerings.

### Kenji

Age: 9

Occupation: Village boy

Personality: Curious, restless, quick-eared, imaginative

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A young friend and occasional student whose discoveries often reach Masaru-sensei before adult notice does.

Continuity notes: Loves birds, insects, clouds, animal tracks, bark boats, bridges, and streams. His curiosity may lead to trouble, but never malice.

### Mika

Age: About 28

Occupation: Young mother and household worker

Personality: Practical, warm, hardworking, unsentimental in a kind way

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Neighbor and steady village presence. She offers grounded observations from household and family life.

Continuity notes: Mother of Rin. She often understands care, shelter, food, warmth, and invisible growth.

### Rin

Age: About 5

Occupation: Village child

Personality: Sincere, bright, physically expressive, delighted by small changes

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Mika's daughter; brings him simple discoveries without self-consciousness.

Continuity notes: One of the youngest recurring characters. She experiences each season directly and can notice what adults have stopped seeing.

### Daichi

Age: About 52

Occupation: Rice farmer

Personality: Patient, grounded, methodical, soil-wise

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A respected farmer whose knowledge of soil and field timing often teaches Masaru-sensei.

Continuity notes: Farms the oldest rice terraces in Yuki no Sato. He believes each season begins in the soil before it appears above ground.

### Yuna

Age: About 35

Occupation: Rice grower

Personality: Skilled, disciplined, direct, resilient

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A field teacher in her own right. She often shows him the labor and timing behind seasonal beauty.

Continuity notes: Chiyo's niece. She works in paddies and grain fields, and understands effort, waiting, and harvest.

### Chiyo

Age: About 63

Occupation: Peony grower

Personality: Exacting, generous, unsentimental, deeply experienced

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Fellow cultivator and respected peer. She can challenge his assumptions about abundance and restraint.

Continuity notes: Yuna's aunt and the village's most respected peony grower. She knows that cultivation includes knowing what to remove.

### Aki

Age: About 11

Occupation: Story-seeking village girl

Personality: Imaginative, fast-moving, verbal, wonder-filled

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A lively young companion who pulls him toward discoveries with urgency and delight.

Continuity notes: Collects stories the way others collect stones. Older sister of Sora.

### Sora

Age: About 7

Occupation: Village child

Personality: Bold, impulsive, curious, sometimes competitive

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A younger child who tests the edges of what she is told.

Continuity notes: Younger sister of Aki. Use sparingly as a bright counterpoint to Aki's story-seeking.

### Jiro

Age: About 74

Occupation: Elder fisherman

Personality: Spare with words, observant, dryly amused, patient

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: River teacher and elder friend. He often knows what the water is doing before anyone else.

Continuity notes: Knows currents, fish behavior, birds, weather, pond, ferry landing, and riverbank signs.

### Ren

Age: About 43

Occupation: Traveling scholar, mapmaker, seasonal observer

Personality: Thoughtful, learned, curious, slightly road-worn

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Intellectual companion whose travels place Yuki no Sato's seasons in a wider world.

Continuity notes: Travels much of the year but returns to Yuki no Sato, especially in spring. Carries maps, notes, and a canvas satchel.

### Osamu

Age: About 48

Occupation: Gardener and caretaker of iris beds

Personality: Careful, patient, quietly proud of well-tended growth

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Practical peer in cultivation.

Continuity notes: Best used around water iris, pond edges, garden maintenance, and the difference between wildness and care.

### Kiku

Age: About 50

Occupation: Village cook

Personality: Warm, brisk, sensory, generous, practical

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Friend who reads the seasons through ingredients and feeding others.

Continuity notes: Measures the year by market baskets, bamboo shoots, plums, citrus, preserved foods, and kitchen work.

### Mei

Age: About 39

Occupation: Lantern maker

Personality: Quiet, perceptive, composed, comfortable with silence

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Companion in subtle forms of light and darkness.

Continuity notes: Makes lanterns for homes, festivals, and ceremonies. Useful in firefly, cricket, evening, and festival stories.

### Haru

Age: About 10

Occupation: Quiet child and observer of small things

Personality: Still, patient, gentle, intensely attentive

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: A child who often sees hidden lives before Masaru-sensei does.

Continuity notes: Notices insects, frogs, caterpillars, things beneath leaves, and things disappearing underground.

### Aya

Age: About 76

Occupation: Grandmother and silkworm keeper

Personality: Traditional, patient, plainspoken, memory-rich

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Keeper of older village rhythms and domestic seasonal knowledge.

Continuity notes: Keeps silkworms and remembers older ways. Can also appear around winter citrus, hearth, cloth, and household memory.

### Nami

Age: About 31

Occupation: Textile dyer

Personality: Attentive, tactile, patient, sensitive to subtle color

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Craft peer who understands transformation through material.

Continuity notes: Works with natural colors, cotton, safflower, cloth, water, and drying.

### Yori

Age: About 24

Occupation: Young farmer

Personality: Earnest, impatient, hardworking, still learning restraint

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Younger villager whose learning mirrors seasonal ripening.

Continuity notes: Often concerned with timing. He is still learning that fullness is not completion.

### Sayo

Age: About 57

Occupation: Herbalist

Personality: Precise, calm, practical, deeply knowledgeable

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Plant-wise colleague who understands healing, drying, gathering, and cycles of usefulness.

Continuity notes: Use for herbs, dew, self-heal, winter greens, gathering, drying, and remedies.

### Saburo

Age: About 46

Occupation: Bamboo craftsman

Personality: Durable, good-humored, resilient, hands-on

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Craft friend whose bamboo work offers lessons in flexibility and recovery.

Continuity notes: Makes baskets, tools, bamboo spouts, and repairs. He embodies strength through bending.

### Sister Rei

Age: About 44

Occupation: Temple attendant

Personality: Serene, disciplined, present, gently direct

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Reflective companion at the temple and pond. She shares routine without turning spiritual practice into spectacle.

Continuity notes: Maintains seasonal observances, temple grounds, equinox preparations, and lotus routines.

### Toma

Age: About 17

Occupation: Aspiring traveler

Personality: Restless, searching, sincere, easily pulled toward distance

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Young villager who brings questions of leaving, return, and belonging.

Continuity notes: Often found on hills, overlooks, or paths toward mountain passes.

### Kenta

Age: About 29

Occupation: Shepherd

Personality: Independent, weather-aware, quiet, comfortable with open spaces

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Hill-country observer who notices wind, fog, clouds, grasses, and distant weather.

Continuity notes: Spends most days above Yuki no Sato. He may understand wind before others feel it.

### Gen

Age: About 61

Occupation: Persimmon orchard keeper

Personality: Steady, seasoned, accepting, practical

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Orchard peer who trusts timing and expected change.

Continuity notes: Has tended the village orchard for more than thirty years. Best used with frost, rain-dark leaves, persimmons, fences, and harvest.

### Goro

Age: About 55

Occupation: Woodcutter

Personality: Strong, blunt, winter-wise, observant in the forest

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Forest guide and practical teacher about cold, rest, fuel, and endurance.

Continuity notes: Works near the forest edge and understands winter preparations.

### Isao

Age: About 42

Occupation: Hunter and path keeper

Personality: Responsible, quiet, capable, preventive rather than dramatic

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Maintains the paths Masaru-sensei walks; teaches the value of unseen responsibility.

Continuity notes: Maintains paths, bridges, and roads. Can appear with snow routes, antlers, mountain edges, and village safety.

### Emi

Age: About 20

Occupation: Newlywed household member

Personality: Hopeful, uncertain, diligent, tender

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Younger neighbor learning belonging in the eastern side of the village.

Continuity notes: Recently married into a village family. Best used for nesting, home-making, and learning a new place.

### Nobu

Age: About 68

Occupation: Retired teacher

Personality: Wise, observant, measured, quietly melancholy but hopeful

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Educated elder and conversational peer.

Continuity notes: Taught village children for nearly forty years. Notices what is ending as closely as what is beginning.

### Taku

Age: About 85

Occupation: Village elder

Personality: Very old, calm, grateful, spare, memory-filled

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: Elder whose long view of arrivals and departures balances Masaru-sensei's daily attentiveness.

Continuity notes: One of the oldest villagers. Appears near river, pine, migration, winter calls, and farewells.

### Entire Village

Age: Multigenerational

Occupation: Community

Personality: Separate in work, joined by seasonal moments

Relationship with Masaru-sensei: The wider human field in which his practice lives.

Continuity notes: Use for cherry blossoms, fireflies, festivals, planting, harvest, storms, and shared seasonal labor.

## Village Relationship Map

- Masaru-sensei is connected to every villager through observation, teaching, flower arranging, walking paths, and seasonal exchange.
- Hana anchors the river crossing and village memory.
- Kenji, Aki, Sora, Haru, and Rin form the child-observer circle; each notices a different kind of first sign.
- Mika is Rin's mother and a practical bridge between household life and village work.
- Daichi, Yuna, and Yori anchor rice, wheat, soil, fields, and harvest timing.
- Chiyo is Yuna's aunt and a cultivation peer to Masaru-sensei.
- Jiro anchors the river, pond, fish, current, and old ferry landing.
- Ren connects Yuki no Sato to neighboring villages, maps, migration, and wider seasonal knowledge.
- Kiku, Aya, Nami, Sayo, Saburo, Mei, and Gen anchor domestic and craft knowledge: food, silk, dye, herbs, bamboo, lanterns, and orchards.
- Sister Rei and Nobu anchor temple, education, reflection, silence, and memory.
- Kenta, Goro, Isao, and Toma anchor hillside, forest, paths, weather, winter labor, and the desire to travel beyond the village.
- The entire village gathers at seasonal thresholds: cherry blossoms, fireflies, planting, harvest, storms, snow, and early spring signs.

## Storytelling Guidelines

Each Ko story should be built around a seasonal sign, not a plot problem. The story may include a question, surprise, hesitation, or small misunderstanding, but it should not depend on conflict as its engine.

Masaru-sensei may observe the sign, be shown the sign, almost miss the sign, misunderstand its timing, or learn its meaning from someone else. Rotate this balance across stories.

Every story should include concrete sensory observation: texture, temperature, scent, weight, color, sound, water, soil, light, or movement.

Ikebana should be grounded in actual material from the story. Arrangements may include cultivated flowers, grasses, branches, reeds, seed pods, leaves, moss, stones, or weathered material. Avoid arrangements that feel decorative only. Each arrangement should reveal relationship, timing, restraint, impermanence, or seasonal transition.

Lessons should be quiet and earned. Prefer simple lines drawn from the seen moment. Avoid lectures, aphorism overload, mysticism, or universal claims that feel too large for the scene.

Village characters should recur naturally. They should not appear merely to admire Masaru-sensei. Each should possess a domain of knowledge that can lead, correct, or deepen his attention.

The stories may be hopeful without forcing cheerfulness. Loss, departure, fading, rest, and endings are welcome when held gently.

## Continuity Rules

- Masaru-sensei is about 60 throughout the annual cycle.
- Masaru-sensei lives on the edge of Yuki no Sato in a modest home with an attached or adjacent flower studio and seasonal garden.
- Masaru-sensei is a respected flower teacher, not "the protagonist" in canon language.
- "Hana no Sensei" and "Flower Teacher" refer to Masaru-sensei.
- His samurai-class education and former service to a local lord are understated background only.
- Do not turn stories toward samurai conflict, warfare, politics, status drama, or hidden heroic identity.
- Yuki no Sato remains Edo-period in atmosphere and material culture.
- The same villagers should reappear across the year according to their domains of knowledge.
- Masaru-sensei should not always be the first to notice, the most knowledgeable, or the final authority.
- Preserve existing public story tone: seasonal observation, nature, community, impermanence, reflection, and hope.
- Keep place geography consistent: river crossing, bridge, old ferry landing, lower stream, rice terraces, fields, orchard, temple, bamboo grove, hillside, forest edge, rocky overlook, and Masaru-sensei's garden.
- The seasons are the true central theme.
